Mastermind Deal Breakdown — Solvang, CA

How We Acquired a 24-Unit Boutique Hotel with Creative Financing

A full walkthrough of the deal structure, capital stack, stabilization playbook, and lessons learned — for operators who are actively buying.

24 hotel rooms plus a 3-bed/3-bath duplex on 0.97 acres in Solvang, California — "The Danish Capital of America." Two hotel buildings plus the adjacent owner's residence, purchased together.

03 — How We Found It

This was a true off-market find. My two partners and I split the country into territories and were actively sourcing deals. We got in touch with this seller directly — she wanted $8.4M. We ran the numbers, told her it didn't work at that price, and walked away.

Then she had health issues. That changed everything. Suddenly she was motivated to sell and willing to negotiate. Because we'd kept the relationship warm, we were the ones she called back. We came in at $5.9M — $2.5M below her original ask — and she took it.

The CoStar Discrepancy

One of the edges we had in negotiation: we discovered CoStar's revenue data for the market was off. When we ran our own analysis, we found the market was generating roughly 5% more revenue than what CoStar showed.

That 5% matters. It meant we could underwrite higher revenue than competing buyers who took the CoStar numbers at face value — which meant we could justify paying more while still hitting our return targets.

The Human Element

The seller lived in the house next door. She wasn't just selling a hotel — she was retiring from her life's work and her home. We structured the deal so she could stay on the property through the end of the year.

That flexibility mattered to her more than squeezing the last dollar out of the price. Understanding what the other side actually needs is how you get deals done that others can't.

Patience is a strategy. We walked away, stayed in touch, and were ready when the situation changed. The best off-market deals don't close on your timeline — they close when the seller is ready.
04 — Due Diligence Checklist

Here is exactly what we verified before closing. Steal this for your next deal.

📊

Market Analysis

  • STR Report: comparable hotel ADR and occupancy
  • CoStar sales comps (and cross-checked against actuals)
  • Solvang TOT Report: 70% market average occupancy
  • Airbnb supply check: only 9 residential STR permits citywide
  • Annual visitor count (1.5M+), event calendar, seasonal pattern
🏗

Property Analysis

  • Physical inspection of all 24 rooms and duplex
  • Seller P&L review (and verified against STR data)
  • Renovation scope and cost estimate ($900K budget)
  • Title search and zoning verification
  • Land use review for 296 1st St duplex (adjacent to hotel zone)
💰

Financial Analysis

  • Cap rate analysis: NOI / cap rate = value (commercial, not comps)
  • Price per door vs. sales comps ($196K vs $218K)
  • DSCR and cash-on-cash return at each financing scenario
  • Refinance projection at stabilized NOI ($9.5M)
  • Sensitivity: does the deal still work at 55% occupancy?
⚖️

Legal & Structural

  • Entity structure (LLC, operating agreement, GP/LP split)
  • Seller financing terms verified in writing before LOI
  • Subject-to mortgage review (3.99% rate, 30-yr fixed)
  • PPM and investor documents reviewed by securities attorney
  • Land use attorney consulted on duplex entitlement path
The CoStar cross-check is the one most buyers skip. Pull your own STR data, call local operators, and build your own occupancy model. That 5% discrepancy we found is the kind of edge that changes whether you can pay the price that closes the deal.

06 — Negotiating the Seller Financing

The mistake most buyers make: they treat seller financing as a concession — something you ask for when you can't qualify. We came at it from the opposite direction.

💰

We Showed Up with Capital

$3M raised, 20%+ down. The seller knew we weren't asking because we were broke. We were asking because the math demanded it.

🤝

Framed as Value for the Seller

Seller financing at 5% generates better yield than cash sitting idle. We helped him see it as an investment he was making — not a favor he was doing us.

📐

Walked Through the Math Live

We showed him the deal model. "Without these terms, there is no deal. With them, here's what you earn and here's our exit plan." Transparent beats clever every time.

Favorable terms are not a sign of weakness. They are how you structure a deal that works for everyone at the table. If you can't close without them, say so — and show why the seller should want that too.
07 — Stabilization Playbook

We ran two tracks simultaneously — revenue optimization and capital improvement — sequenced intentionally by season.

Busy Season Strategy

Revenue First, Renovations Later

  • Maximized revenue without touching the asset
  • Implemented dynamic pricing
  • Improved marketing and distribution channels
  • Pushed direct bookings to reduce OTA fees
  • Began group and events pipeline

Slow Season Execution

Capital Improvements

  • Phase 1 (Apr-Sept 2024): Exterior paint, linen refresh, landscaping renovation, ops handoff
  • Phase 2 (Oct 2024-Feb 2025): Full interior renovation, rebrand launch, local partnerships
  • $900K renovation budget with 20% contingency reserves

The GC Management System

Renovation chaos is expensive. We pre-loaded everything before the GC showed up.

🖼

Printed Room Images

Physical printed images of the target finish were taped inside each room. Zero ambiguity about what "done" looks like.

📦

FF&E Organized by Room

Furniture, fixtures, and equipment itemized and labeled per room before a single nail was pulled.

🚛

Everything On-Site Before Start

All product ordered and staged in containers on-site ahead of the work start date. No waiting on deliveries mid-project.

08 — Lessons Learned
📋

Get Ahead of Permitting

We would have hired a land use consultant earlier in the process. Getting in front of entitlement questions before you're under pressure is cheaper and faster than reacting to enforcement risk mid-project.

🤖

Use AI for Regulatory Sentiment

We fed city council meeting minutes into AI to understand not just how members would vote, but how they felt and what language resonated with each of them. We walked in knowing the temperature of the room. That's not something a consultant can give you the same way.

🎯

Define Role Ownership Up Front

On a multi-partner deal, be ruthlessly clear about who owns what — and group responsibilities by category, not by task. The goal is minimum overlap between partners.

Roles that seem separate will eventually collide. Legal structure and capital raising look independent until you're drafting investor documents — then suddenly both partners are in the same lane. Design the team structure so that happens as rarely as possible, and name a single person accountable for the outcome of each category.
09 — Key Takeaways for Active Deal Buyers
01

Creative deal structure can make or break a deal

Conventional financing would have killed this deal. Seller financing, subject-to, and private money layered together made the numbers work. If you only know one way to structure a deal, you'll miss most of the opportunities in front of you.

02

Renovations require an outrageous level of precision

The difference between a smooth renovation and a disaster is almost entirely preparation. Printed room specs, FF&E organized by room, product on-site before work starts. Every dollar of chaos costs two dollars to fix.

03

AI for the boring stuff is a massive edge

Legal documents, permitting, planning — the stuff nobody wants to read. AI gets through it faster and catches what humans miss. Feeding council meeting minutes into AI gave us intelligence that no consultant could match on the same timeline.

04

Experienced operators make everything easier

Having a partner with a real track record isn't just about reducing downside risk. It makes lenders more comfortable, makes raising capital easier, and opens doors that are closed to first-timers. Experience compounds across every part of the deal.
