POST 3:
HOOK: Most STR investors have no idea the SBA will finance a micro resort.

Here's how it works.

SBA 7(a) loan. Up to $5M. 10-year term on real estate deals. Down payment as low as 10%.

For a $1.5M micro resort, that's $150k down — not $375k like a conventional 25% down deal.

The asset has to generate enough revenue to service the debt. That's the underwrite. If the numbers work, the SBA cares less about your personal income than a conventional lender does.

Compare that to DSCR loans, which are designed for residential STRs and cap out well before commercial hospitality territory.

STR to micro resort is not just a mindset shift — it's a financing shift. Once you understand the capital stack, deals in the $500k-$2M range become accessible.

I'm currently under contract on a micro resort using SBA financing. This is not theory.

HOOK: I almost passed on a deal because the trailing 12 numbers looked terrible.

Here's what I missed — and what it taught me about underwriting hospitality.

The property had 8 cabins, a great location, and was priced below replacement cost. But the occupancy was 38% and the reviews were brutal.

My first read: distressed asset, too much operational risk, pass.

What I didn't look at: every bad review was about the same three things. Broken AC units. Unresponsive management. A check-in process that hadn't been updated in 5 years.

None of those are location problems. They're operations problems — and operations can be fixed.

When I underwrote it as a stabilized asset at 60% occupancy with updated rates and a clean guest experience, the numbers worked.

STR investors are trained to underwrite demand. Hospitality investors underwrite management.

The question isn't "is this market strong?" — it's "is this asset being operated well?"

POST 5:
HOOK: Not every market can support a micro resort. Here's exactly what I look for.

4 filters I run before I look at a single deal in a market:

1. Drive distance from a major metro.
Two hours or less. People will drive 90 minutes for a weekend. They won't fly for it. Your repeat guest pool has to be reachable without a flight.

2. An experiential anchor.
Lake, river, mountains, national park, wine region, something. The resort doesn't create the draw — it captures it. If there's nothing to do outside your property, you're fighting for demand instead of riding it.

3. Limited branded hotel competition.
If Marriott and Hilton have already saturated the market, you're competing with their loyalty programs and their OTA spend. Look for markets where the inventory is fragmented — small independent operators who haven't invested in brand or experience.

4. Land-to-key ratio that pencils.
Not every beautiful piece of land supports the density you need to hit your returns. Run the numbers on a per-key basis before you fall in love with the acreage.

These four filters eliminate probably 70% of the markets I look at before I pull a single deal off the MLS.
